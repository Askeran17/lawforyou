In the Code of Procedure, where there were provisions regarding the manner of pursuing an action against a lower court's decision, it was stated in which cases what should be used. This was, with certain exceptions, partly when appealing lower court decisions in civil cases of such a nature that they should be brought by summons, partly when suing against lower court decisions in certain cases, which belonged to the extraordinary civil process. According to the Code of Procedure, when a case was decided by a lower court or the court ruled that the case should be sworn, the court would indicate in the decision whether the completion of the claim would instead take place by way of appeal or appeal, the other of the then two legal remedies that could come into question. If someone wanted to complain about another decision issued by a lower court, the court or the district head would, upon request, send him a referral for enforcement. The pledge procedure, which was stipulated in the Code of Procedure, began with the dissatisfied party, upon payment of a fixed pledge, notifying that he wanted to appeal; which was to be done no later than the seventh day from the day the decision was given and filed with the court or the person appointed to receive such notification on behalf of the court. After what was paid, proof of this was signed on the decision with notification of the time within which the appellant would submit a pledge to the Court of Appeal. On request, the other party would receive proof of the bet and notice of the notice period.
